## 6. Synthesis
## 1. Conclusion
Based on the available randomized controlled trials (RCTs), the use of implant-retained overdentures (IODs) is associated with significantly better patient-reported outcomes, including higher denture satisfaction and improved oral health-related quality of life (OHRQoL), compared to conventional relines, particularly in very old edentulous patients. Functionally, IOD placement significantly improved maximum voluntary bite force and masseter muscle thickness. While chewing efficiency was not found to be significantly different between the groups in one study, the evidence suggests that IODs provide superior functional stability and quality of life measures compared to traditional removable dentures. However, the certainty of this conclusion is limited by the consistent reports of potential detection and performance bias across the included studies.

## 2. Summary of Evidence
Two key RCTs were analyzed.

The first RCT (PMID: 24158342) included 34 very old edentulous patients (aged 75 years or older). The intervention group (n=16) received placement of 2 interforaminal short implants loaded with Locator attachments to create implant-retained overdentures (IODs). The control group (n=18) received a conventional reline of their existing lower denture. Outcomes were measured at 3 months and 12 months. This study reported that IODs resulted in significantly higher denture satisfaction and increased OHRQoL compared to the control group. Functionally, the intervention group showed a significant improvement in maximum voluntary bite force and increased masseter muscle thickness. Chewing efficiency was not statistically different between the two groups.

The second RCT (PMID: 24884848) was a prospective multi-center RCT involving 180 edentulous patients (aged 60 to 89). This study compared two implant loading protocols: immediate loading (experimental group) versus delayed loading after 3 months of submerged healing (control group). The primary outcome measured was the non-inferiority of the implant success rate. Secondary outcomes included clinical, technical, and subjective variables, and follow-up was planned for 24 months.

## 3. Confidence in Evidence & GRADE
The overall quality of evidence is moderate, but the certainty of the conclusion is limited by methodological concerns across the included studies. Both primary studies utilized robust RCT designs with proper randomization procedures (rated as Low risk of selection bias).

However, the risk of bias was assessed as "Some Concerns" in several critical domains:
1. Performance Bias: In the IOD study, participants were aware of whether they were receiving implants or conventional relines, which limits the ability to blind participants.
2. Detection Bias: In both RCTs, there was no explicit confirmation that the outcome evaluators were blinded to the treatment group assignment, raising the risk of detection bias.
3. Attrition Bias: The studies were noted to have limitations in providing detailed plans for handling missing data.

These limitations in blinding and measurement introduce caution, meaning the strong positive findings regarding functional and subjective outcomes observed in the IOD study must be interpreted with the understanding that bias was possible.

## 4. Clinical Implications
The findings strongly suggest that implant-supported overdentures offer significant functional and quality-of-life advantages over conventional complete or partial dentures for aged edentulous patients, particularly in the mandible. Clinicians should consider IOD placement when treating older patients who require improved masticatory function and who may benefit from enhanced oral health-related quality of life. For surgeons, the evidence highlights the importance of using well-established implant systems and protocols to maximize stability and functional outcomes. However, due to the identified bias risks, these findings should guide clinical decision-making but not replace the need for further, ideally blinded, long-term research.
